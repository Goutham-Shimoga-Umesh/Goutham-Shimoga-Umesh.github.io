VOF Gas Leak with Cross-Flow — Transient Dispersion (2D)

Multiphase (VOF) + buoyancy + turbulence — safety/dispersion scenario
VOFMultiphase TransientSafety/Dispersion
Methane plume, two inlet/outlet configurations (Task 1a/1b) · ANSYS Fluent 2024 R2

Why this case. Gas-leak dispersion is an industry-relevant safety problem. It couples mixture transport, buoyancy and interface capturing. Here I model a methane release into a cross-flow using the Volume of Fluid (VOF) method and compare two boundary setups.

What’s shown. Methane volume fraction at t = 3.0 s and t = 4.5 s for both cases, plus the mesh/time-stepping details used to reach clean convergence.

Model & Numerics
Physics VOF (two phases): methane (light) in air; gravity/buoyancy enabled; transient, pressure-based solver
Turbulence RANS (teaching setup); cross-flow provides background shear
Mesh size ≈ 182,500 elements; ≈ 184,351 nodes
Time stepping Δt = 0.01 s; ≤ 60 iterations/step
Output instants 3.0 s (≈300 steps) and 4.5 s (≈450 steps)
Boundary-Condition Sets Compared
Case Inlet D Outlet A Notes
1a — Case A Pressure inlet, gauge 100 Pa Pressure outlet Baseline release into cross-flow
1b — Case B Pressure inlet, gauge 250 Pa Velocity outlet; u(y)=0.6y − 0.02y² Stronger driving pressure and parabolic outlet profile → longer, asymmetric plume
Case A — methane volume fraction at t = 3.0 s. Twin counter-rotating roll-up forms from the cap of the jet; plume remains compact.
Case A — t = 4.5 s. Coherent eddies grow while the cap lifts; footprint still symmetric and shorter than Case B.
Case B — t = 3.0 s. Higher inlet pressure and outlet velocity profile tilt the plume; one lobe expands faster.
Case B — t = 4.5 s. Stronger advection/entrainment: longer tongue, thicker shear layer, and more pronounced asymmetry.

Takeaways. Case B’s stronger driving and velocity-specified outlet stretch the plume and enhance asymmetry, a typical plant-layout sensitivity. Even on a modest grid and Δt, VOF captures coherent roll-up and buoyant rise cleanly.
